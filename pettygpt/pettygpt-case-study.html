Case Study

I Felt Petty
So I Designed
an App.

PettyGPT · Capstone → Refined · 4 weeks (across 2 years)
The pitch · 30 seconds

PettyGPT is a tone-controlled reply generator. Paste in a message that bugged you. Pick a tone — Corporate Cold, Drama Queen, Karen Mode, Zen Shade. The bot writes the reply you wish you had thought of.

Built originally as my capstone in 2025 — Revived in 2026 as a fully working prototype with real tone categorization, ten distinct voices, and a brand voice that takes itself just seriously enough.

Why this exists

I'm a chronic reply-deleter.

About a year ago I realized something embarrassing about myself: the version of me that exists in my head writes much sharper replies than the version of me with thumbs. I'd type something perfect, hover, delete it, send something diplomatic, and then walk around for forty minutes thinking about what I should have actually sent.

I'm not malicious. I'm just petty — in the way most chronically-polite people are. The reply that lands in my brain is calibrated, witty, and slightly devastating. The reply that leaves my phone is "ok!"

My capstone brief was open-ended. I didn't want to do a typical dashboard. So I designed an app for the version of me that exists in my head — the one that finishes the thought.

"An app for the perfect reply you'd send if your better judgment took a five-minute break."
2025 · The capstone

The first version was pretty, but it wasn't petty.

The original PettyGPT was a Figma prototype with all the right vibes and none of the actual brain. Warm peach palette, soft gradient blob, glassmorphic cards. I shipped it as a clickable Figma, got the grade, and moved on.

The honest reason it stopped there: integrating AI in 2025 was harder than it sounds. I wasn't a developer, and the tooling for designers to wire up a screen into a working prototype was tough. So PettyGPT lived in Figma — looking great, doing nothing.

Original · Splash

The splash — wordmark, a big gradient blob, a soft pill CTA. Pretty, but wellness-app territory.

Original · Compose

The compose screen. Mono-typed "Be Nice? Nope." with a glassy prompt card and a soft mic.

Looking back at it now, the bigger problem wasn't the missing AI. It was that the design was treating petty like polite. Soft pastel palette. Friendly buttons. I had built a wellness app for grudges.

2026 · The revival

So I came back to it.

The thing I couldn't pull off in 2025, actually generating the replies, became trivially easy with the new tooling. I had a version of me with thumbs that could finally write the sharp reply. PettyGPT deserved a second pass, this time as a real product.

I also wanted to put something on my portfolio that had real product judgment in it, not just craft. Something that showed I could spot a problem in my own work and rebuild around it. PettyGPT had the ideal arc: I made it, I shelved it, I came back to it knowing more.

The redesign

I scrapped the wellness vibe. Went editorial.

The biggest single decision was the new design language. PettyGPT isn't sweet, it's sharp. So instead of pastels and gradients, I leaned into a contemporary editorial direction — bone background, near-black ink, one accent red, italic Instrument Serif as the voice, JetBrains Mono for metadata. No glassmorphism, no blob, no soft shadows. The kind of typography you'd see on a magazine cover, not on a productivity tool.

Excessive tone — cards shift to blush with a softer coral accent.

The cards repaint based on which tone you pick.

This is the design move I'm most happy about. Instead of giving every tone the same surface, I split the ten tones into two moods:

Subtle tones — Corporate Cold, Overly Polite, Corporate Burn, Deadpan Savage, The Ghoster, Zen Shade — sit on a cream card.

Excessive tones — Drama Queen, Sassy Friend, Karen Mode, Petty Poet — sit on a blush card. The whole interface repaints when you switch. The product literally looks like the tone you've picked.

"Calm Karen Mode and calm Corporate Cold should not occupy the same visual space. The card color tells you what kind of petty you're about to send."
A small interaction I'm proud of

The tone selector is a card that opens itself.

Standard pattern would be: tap a select control, get a modal, pick from a list, modal closes. That's three context shifts. Instead, the tone card nests its own list inside itself. Tap the round black dropdown next to the tone name and the card stretches downward to reveal all ten options inline.

Each row in the list has a tiny color dot — ink for subtle tones, red for excessive — so you can scan for the kind of pettiness you want before committing.

Making it feel like a real app

Halfway through the rebuild I realized the app felt thin. A reply generator is one screen of utility — you can't put that on a portfolio and call it a product. So I added the screens that turn a tool into a destination:

A detail that makes me happy

The loading screen has opinions.

When you tap "Get Petty," there's a brief loading moment while the bot drafts. Instead of a generic spinner, I integrated a lazy loading experience:

calibrating shade…
consulting the spite archive…
drafting with extreme prejudice…
sharpening your tongue…
channeling your worst thoughts…
aligning the perfect comeback…

Loading screens are usually a wasted moment. This one tells you what kind of product you're using before you've even seen the reply.

Reflections

What this taught me.

Iteration is key. The splash alone went through five versions before this one stuck. Three years ago I'd have stopped at the first.

Brand voice is a system, not a vibe. Once I committed to one accent red and italic serif as the personality, every screen wrote itself.

Density makes the difference. History, favorites, hall of fame — none of it improves the core reply. All of it makes the app feel lived in.

Come back to the work. The 2025 version wasn't bad — it just wasn't ready. Some projects only become themselves on the second pass.
