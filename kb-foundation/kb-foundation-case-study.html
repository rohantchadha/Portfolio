— The problem
A free-education site that read like a fundraising appeal.

The old website opened with three taglines stacked down the page: "We have faith in raising a generation of empowered souls." "We have faith in a better quality of life for young kids." "We have faith in YOU." It closed with a Support Us section listing school fees, laptops, and teacher salaries.

Nowhere on the page could a student find a course, a schedule, or a way to enrol. The audience was donors. The traffic, by then, was students — KB Foundation had grown to 150+ learners across 8 courses while the website still spoke past them.

The brief wasn't an audience problem. It was the wrong primary audience.

Old website · 2021
— The decision
The decision wasn't how it looks. It was who it's for.

If a free-education non-profit doesn't lead with its courses, it's a brochure. Once you decide the homepage is for not just a donor but a student too, the rest of the site rearranges itself. Courses become the spine. Mission statements become history. The team becomes a page you can actually reach. Donate becomes a pill in the corner — present, never loud. From that one decision, three concrete moves followed.

Language. The vocabulary changed from charity statements to operational facts. Out went mission, vision, values, and 'we have faith in you'. In came course names with durations and tools, educator bios with credentials, event dates, named testimonials, and volunteer roles you could actually apply for.

Hierarchy. The homepage now answers a student's questions in the order a student would ask them — what is this, is it real, how does it work, what can I learn, who else trusts it. The donate path runs in parallel: a pill in the persistent nav, a full page when clicked. Neither audience has to scroll past the other.

Trust. The team came up from a buried "For more information get in touch with…" line to a full About page with four core members and five educator bios — credentials, faces, qualifications. On a free site, students need a reason to believe the teachers are real.

— The kit
Six pages, one vocabulary.

The brief was a website, not a rebrand — the logo stayed. The visual vocabulary built around it spans six pages: Montserrat for every line of type, a family of pastel card tints that vary section to section but share one palette logic, two semantic colours for utility navigation (lavender for Donate, pink for Contact), and a documentary photography ethos that runs through every hero.

— Homepage
The whole offer, in one scroll.

The homepage opens with "Education That Opens Doors" against a gradient hero and a single CTA — an About Us pill — and immediately below, in the same fold, the three numbers that matter: 150+ students, 8 courses, 5 educators. That stat row is the first thing the old site didn't have. It tells a student arriving from a WhatsApp link that this is a real operation, not a pamphlet.

From there the page narrates the offer in order: How Learning Works (five blocks — 100% online, small batches, multi-level, tests, certificates), the Refundable Learning Model, a horizontal scroll of Our Programs, Our Community event cards (Diwali get-together, MS Office certification batch), three student testimonials, and a Make a Difference CTA that hands volunteers off cleanly. Donate sits where it always sits — a small pastel pill, top-right.

— A product decision
A refundable deposit — the only money on a free site.

Free programs have a dropout problem. When a student pays nothing to enrol, there's nothing to lose by ghosting in week three. KB Foundation already had a workaround — a small refundable deposit returned on completion — but it was buried in a WhatsApp message, not on the website. We made it a homepage section.

The design treats it as a feature, not fine print. Three pillars, one line each. The language is plain on purpose — students need to see, immediately, that the deposit is fully refundable and that there are no hidden fees.

Fully Refundable

The deposit is returned in full after successful completion of the course.

Encourages Commitment

Helps students stay consistent through the program and finish what they started.

No Hidden Charges

There are no course fees. Only a refundable security deposit.

— Programs
The page the old site didn't have.

The old website had no Programs page. Courses didn't exist as a destination — just a mission statement, a list of values, and a Support Us pitch at the bottom. Adding the page is the move that reframes the whole foundation: a charity has a mission, an EdTech has a syllabus. A student now arrives and finds the offer in one place; a donor sees what they'd be funding; a volunteer sees what they'd be teaching. The foundation has been adding new courses to the page on its own since handoff.

— About & team
A trust funnel, top to bottom.

The hardest UX problem on a free-education site is belief. A student about to give six months of evenings to a course they don't pay for needs to believe the teachers exist, the foundation is real, and the people running it are qualified. The old site addressed none of it — the whole site was a single-page brochure, and the only team mention was a contact line tucked under the "Support Us" donate ask. The new About page is structured as a trust funnel: belief, then approach, then leadership, then teachers. By the end of the scroll, a student knows whether to trust the people, not the slogan.

— Donate
A donor's page. Off the student's path.

The old website tried to convince a donor and onboard a student in the same scroll — "Support Us" was the only real CTA, sitting at the bottom of the same page meant to introduce the foundation. The new site separates the two paths. Donate gets its own destination, reachable from the persistent nav at any moment. A donor who lands here gets a focused page.

— Volunteer
Filter before the form.

Volunteers are how KB Foundation scales — the teachers, the mentors, the people who answer WhatsApp on a Wednesday evening. The Volunteer page is built around the single question a prospective volunteer arrives with: "what would I actually do?"

The page is built to let volunteers filter themselves before they apply. Three categories, one line each, no form on the page itself — the form lives one click away. A volunteer reads the three lines, decides whether they fit, then clicks through. The foundation gets applications from people who've already decided they belong here.

— Contact
No form. An invitation.

The Contact page is "Lets Talk.", an email, and a phone number. No form, no dropdown for "What is this regarding?", no auto-responder. The foundation is small enough that any message goes to a person, not a queue, and the page should say so by being almost empty.

— A small thing
Where Kunaal ended up.

KB Foundation was started in memory of Kunaal Bhatia. On the old website, the dedication ran across the top of the page and set the whole tone — the site was, first, a memorial. We moved the dedication to the footer of every page and enlarged it.

Same words, different position, different job. The site is now about the students Kunaal would have wanted — not about Kunaal.

What the redesign actually shifted.

Real UX shifts the redesign made — each one solving a problem the old site couldn't address.

— Reflection

A brochure became a working website.

The old site was a single-page brochure asking for donations. I turned it into a proper website — one that still does the NGO things (donations, volunteer recruitment) but also actually serves the students it was built for. Same foundation. Three working paths where there was one.
