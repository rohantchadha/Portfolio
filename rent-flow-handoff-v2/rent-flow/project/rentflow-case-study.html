One paragraph, then we begin.

A year ago I designed RentFlow at a 10k Designers bootcamp — Figma, wireframes, the whole rite of passage. Last month I rebuilt it without a single wireframe. ChatGPT did the brand visuals. Claude did the thinking. Stitch did the screens. The question I want this page to answer: did AI make me faster, sharper, or just very productive at the wrong things?

Three tools, one job each.

No tool wore more than one hat. The clarity came from picking the right tool for each step rather than asking one model to do everything.

ChatGPT

Brand imagery, hero compositions, the things Stitch can’t render — bloom, glow, soft material light.

Claude

UX critique, copy, prompt authoring, and the case-study page you’re reading right now.

Google Stitch

High-fidelity screen assembly. Replaces the wireframe step entirely — prompts in, hi-fi out.

From eighteen screens to ten.

I built the bootcamp version to ask for email, Aadhaar, payment date, roommates, and autopay — all before the user saw their property. The rebuild flips the order. After the property code, the property page loads first — landlord, building, rent, due date already filled in. Then the user's name. That's it. Email and social login are gone; Indian fintech runs on phone OTP, not Google or Facebook. The payment date is the landlord's call. Roommates moved to the Splits tab. Autopay moved to the dashboard, where it's an opt-in after the user trusts the app.

New · 2026

Both flows start with a property code. What happens next is where they split.

The previous version asked the user for their name, email, and Aadhaar — then showed a basic property page with the landlord's name and address. After that, the tenant picked their own payment date and added their roommates.

The rebuild does the opposite. After the property code, the property page loads first — landlord, building, rent, due date already set by the landlord. The user confirms it's their place, types a first name, and they're in. The old flow asked for everything before showing anything. The new flow shows everything before asking for anything.

New · 2026

The old dashboard tried to entertain. The new one tries to disappear.

The old dashboard treated rent like a game — hourglass, streaks, rewards trophy, “Pay Early.” The rebuild treats it like a financial obligation. Rent, due date, “Pay Now.” Paying rent early isn’t an accomplishment to reward.

Autopay moved from onboarding to an opt-in link under the Pay Now button. The user shouldn’t hand over a UPI mandate before they’ve seen the app work.

Streaks didn’t disappear, just stopped celebrating — “On time, 6 mo” is a factual stat alongside total paid this year and next due. Also actively shows the rent status of the roommates.

Old · Roommate overlay 2024

Send Reminder CTA, John Phillips overdue, age, photo, full payment history.

The old design treated the roommate like a debtor. A Send Reminder CTA, a photo, an age. Splits became a surveillance tool.

The rebuild keeps the visibility — you can still see who’s paid and who hasn’t — and removes the enforcement. Status pills only. No button you can tap to put pressure on another tenant — the Splits widget shows each roommate’s status at a glance, never a way to act on them. The new flow uses Send Proposal, not Send Reminder. You propose a new arrangement, you don’t pressure a person.

Splits is visibility. Accountability is between landlord and tenant — never between roommates.

Receipts, not rewards.

The Payments tab is the user’s record. Each row shows the month, the amount, the method, and a SUCCESS pill — nothing else. It gives out the lease information, highlights the amount the user has paid till now and a countdown to the renewal.

Filters at the top handle the rest of the history. Here the receipt is the record itself, scoped by year, with the payment method preserved.

Memory, not motivation.

All your property information, in one place.

Your home sits at the top — address, monthly rent, and the lease photo. Below it, everything that usually lives in three different emails or a forgotten WhatsApp thread: when you moved in, how long your agreement runs, how much deposit you paid, when it’s refundable. One screen, every term.

If there’s no signed lease yet, RentFlow’s Digital Agreement works as one — verified by RentFlow Shield, viewable in a tap, valid as a record between landlord and tenant.

WhatsApp is not a feature. It’s the spine.

The Landlord Contact screen has Call, Email and a Chat on WhatsApp button — all three with equal visual weight. In a US fintech, email is primary; here, it’s the polite fallback no one will use.

Six things that don’t translate.

Western fintech defaults are a trap. Every one of these came from sitting with the actual flow and asking: would my mom use this?

“Property Manager” → “Landlord.”

The Indian rental market has landlords, not corporate property managers.

Phone OTP as identity.

Muscle memory of PhonePe, CRED, GPay, Paytm. A six-digit code is what trust looks like.

Rent due 1st–5th.

Landlord-set. Tenant doesn’t pick. The app inherits, the user accepts.

Lavender 2024 → Luminous Obsidian.

Pastel-on-white said “friendly app for a small problem.” Rent is a high-stakes monthly category. It deserves the same gravity as the bank app sitting next to it.

The AI smoothened my process. The decisions stayed mine.

Every tool on this page took manual work off my desk. None of them named the user, named the market, or knew when to cut a feature.

What the AI did
  • 01Generated visuals.
  • 02Assembled high-fidelity screens directly from prompts. No wireframe step.
  • 03Iterated copy at scale.
  • 04Surfaced layout options I wouldn’t have hand-sketched.
What I did
  • 01Held the strategic frame across the whole product, not one screen at a time.
  • 02Named the user, named the market, named the constraints (RBI 2FA, UPI, Aadhaar caution).
  • 03Decided what to cut — gamification, social login, the streak system, the Send Reminder CTA.
  • 04Owned the splits philosophy. Visibility, not enforcement.
  • 05Recognised the Indian context and rejected Western fintech defaults.

The AI made me faster. It didn’t make me a better designer. That’s still on me.

Four notes I want to keep.

The AI’s worst trait is enthusiasm.

It will happily build the wrong thing, very fast. My job became knowing when to stop, rethink, and write a better prompt.

Stitch can’t render bloom or glow.

So I generated hero visuals in ChatGPT and let Stitch handle utility screens. Right tool, right step.

Cutting features is harder than adding them.

The old app had a streak system and an Uber Eats redemption flow. Both are gone. Both made the app worse. The AI would not have removed them on its own.

Indian context is non-negotiable.

No Western fintech pattern transferred without re-examination — Aadhaar paranoia, UPI defaults, WhatsApp, landlord vocabulary.
